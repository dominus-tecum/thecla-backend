[0] ## III. Septic Shock ##

[1] **A. Pathophysiology:**

[2] A systemic infection triggers a massive inflammatory response, causing:

[3] 1. Profound Systemic Vasodilation and capillary leak, leading to massive fluid shifts out of the vasculature.

[4] 2. Myocardial Depression from inflammatory mediators.

[5] This results in a combination of low systemic vascular resistance (SVR) and low cardiac output.

[6] **B. Common Causes in Neonates:**

[7] Early-onset or Late-onset Neonatal Sepsis (Bacterial or Viral).

[8] **C. Clinical Presentation (The "Warm Shock" Profile - Early Phase):**

[9] Vasodilation: Bounding pulses, warm extremities initially due to low SVR.

[10] Flash Capillary Refill

[11] Wide Pulse Pressure

[12] Tachycardia

[13] Hypotension

[14] As shock progresses and cardiac output falls, it can evolve into "cold shock" with poor perfusion.

[15] **D. Management:**

[16] Primary Goal: Restore intravascular volume and combat infection.

[17] 1. Aggressive Fluid Resuscitation (First-Line Intervention):

[18] Rapid boluses of 10-20 mL/kg of isotonic crystalloid (Normal Saline). Multiple boluses are often required to fill the dilated, leaky vasculature.

[19] 2. Immediate Empiric Antibiotics:

[20] Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics must be administered as soon as possible after cultures are obtained.

[21] 3. Inotropic/Pressor Support:

[22] Required if hypotension persists despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Norepinephrine is often used to counteract the profound vasodilation.

[23] **Differentiating Cardiogenic and Septic Shock**

[24] The initial clinical presentation provides critical clues for differentiating between cardiogenic and septic shock. Accurate assessment is essential for guiding appropriate, life-saving treatment.

[25] **Cardiogenic Shock Presentation:**

[26] - Primary Problem: Pump failure resulting in low cardiac output

[27] - Peripheral Pulses: Weak and thready

[28] - Skin Temperature: Cold and clammy

[29] - Capillary Refill: Prolonged (>3-4 seconds)

[30] - Key Management Priority: Inotropic support first, with cautious fluid administration

[31] **Septic Shock Presentation (Early Phase):**

[32] - Primary Problem: Vasodilation and capillary leak causing low systemic vascular resistance

[33] - Peripheral Pulses: Bounding

[34] - Skin Temperature: Warm (initially)

[35] - Capillary Refill: Brisk or flash (initially)

[36] - Key Management Priority: Aggressive fluid resuscitation first, followed by antibiotics and vasopressors as needed

[37] **3.3.3. Arrhythmias: Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT), Bradycardia**

[38] Cardiac arrhythmias in neonates, while less common than respiratory or septic issues, can be life-threatening. The neonatal nurse must be able to rapidly recognize and initiate management for the two most clinically significant arrhythmias.

[40] **I. Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)**

[41] SVT is the most common symptomatic tachyarrhythmia in neonates. It involves a rapid, abnormal heart rhythm originating above the ventricles (atria or AV node).

[42] **A. Pathophysiology:**

[43] - Typically caused by a re-entrant circuit, often involving an accessory electrical pathway that creates a short-circuit, causing the heart to beat in a rapid, looped pattern.

[44] **B. Clinical Presentation:**

[45] - Heart Rate: Abrupt onset of a very rapid, very regular heart rate, usually 220-320 beats per minute.

[46] - Infant Behavior: May appear irritable, lethargic, or pale. Signs of poor perfusion develop quickly.

[47] - Cardiovascular Effects: If sustained, leads to poor cardiac output, presenting as signs of congestive heart failure (tachypnea, poor feeding, hepatomegaly) and shock (hypotension, mottling).

[48] **C. Management:**
The approach is determined by the infant's clinical stability.

[49] - Unstable Infant (Signs of Shock, CHF, Altered Consciousness):

[50] - Immediate Synchronized Cardiovascular: The definitive treatment to break the arrhythmia.

[51] - Stable Infant:

[52] 1. Vagal Maneuvers: First-line non-pharmacologic intervention. Applying a bag of ice or a cold, wet washcloth to the infant's forehead and bridge of the nose for 5-10 seconds can stimulate the dive reflex and terminate the SVT. Note: Ocular pressure is contraindicated.

[53] 2. Adenosine: First-line pharmacologic therapy. It is given as a rapid IV push followed immediately by a saline flush. It causes a transient heart block, breaking the re-entrant circuit. The nurse must have cardiac monitoring in place and be prepared for a brief period of asystole or bradycardia.

[54] 3. Long-term Management: If recurrent, medications like Propranolol or Digoxin may be started for maintenance therapy.

[58] **II. Bradycardia**

[59] Bradycardia is defined as a sustained heart rate significantly below the normal for age (e.g., <100 beats per minute for a term neonate, <80 bpm for a preterm infant). It is almost always a symptom of an underlying problem, not a primary diagnosis.

[60] **A. Common Causes:**

[61] - Hypoxemia: This is the most common cause. Any event that causes hypoxia (e.g., apnea, airway obstruction, respiratory failure, seizures) will trigger a vagal response and bradycardia. The mantra "Bradycardia is a late sign of hypoxia" is fundamental.

[62] - Increased Vagal Tone: From procedures like suctioning or feeding tubes.

[63] - Heart Block: A problem with the heart's intrinsic electrical conduction system (e.g., congenital complete heart block).

[64] - Medications: Side effect of drugs like Digoxin.

[65] **B. Clinical Presentation and Management:**
The management is entirely directed at the underlying cause.

[66] Step 1: Stimulate and Assess.

[67] - Provide gentle tactile stimulation.

[68] - Simultaneously assess the infant's color, tone, and respiratory effort.

[69] Step 2: Treat the Underlying Cause.

[70] - If due to hypoxemia/apnea, the priority is to support ventilation and oxygenation with positive pressure ventilation (PPV) via bag-mask. The heart rate will almost always improve once oxygenation is restored.

[71] - If due to a primary cardiac conduction problem (heart block), the infant may require pharmacologic support (e.g., Isoproterenol) or pacemaker placement.

[72] **Key Takeaway for the Nurse:**

[73] For SVT, think "fast and regular." Your rapid assessment of stability determines the immediate action: cardiovert if unstable, try vagal maneuvers/adenosine if stable.

[74] For Bradycardia, think "late sign of hypoxia." Your first response is not to jump to cardiac drugs, but to open the airway and ventilate the infant. Treat the cause, not the number. Your systematic approach (Stimulate → Breathe) is critical for a positive outcome.
